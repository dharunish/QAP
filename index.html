Question-Analysis Prompting Improves LLM Performance in Reasoning Tasks

Dharunish Yugeswardeenoo1, Kevin Zhu2, Sean O'Brien3,

Algoverse AI Research

Published in Proceedings of the 62nd Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics (ACL) @ ACL 2025
arXiv Video Code Data

Abstract

Although LLMs have the potential to transform many fields, they still underperform humans in reasoning tasks. Existing methods induce the model to produce step-by-step calculations, but this research explores the question: Does making the LLM analyze the question improve its performance? We propose a novel prompting strategy called Question-Analysis Prompting (QAP), in which the model is prompted to explain the question in n words before solving. The value of n influences the length of response generated by the model. QAP is evaluated on GPT-3.5 Turbo and GPT-4 Turbo across arithmetic datasets GSM8K, AQuA, and SAT, as well as commonsense dataset StrategyQA. QAP is compared with chain-of-thought (CoT), Plan and Solve Prompting (PS+), and Take A Deep Breath (TADB). QAP outperforms all state-of-the-art prompts on AQuA and SAT datasets for both GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 Turbo, ranking among the top-2 prompts in 75 % of tests. The analysis shows that response length is a critical factor: longer reasoning chains help with harder questions but can harm easy-question accuracy.

Highlights

  • Introduces QAP (Question-Analysis Prompting): a zero-shot prompting method where the model first explains the question (in at least n words) before attempting to solve.
  • The parameter n (word count) is configurable (e.g., 25, 50, 100, 150, 200) and correlates with response length and performance.
  • Evaluation across arithmetic reasoning (datasets: GSM8K, AQuA, SAT) and commonsense reasoning (dataset: StrategyQA) with two model sizes (GPT-3.5 Turbo, GPT-4 Turbo).
  • QAP outperforms or matches other strong prompting baselines — including Chain-of-Thought (CoT), Plan & Solve Plus (PS+), and Take A Deep Breath (TADB) — particularly on harder tasks AQuA and SAT.
  • Finds an interesting trade-off: longer, more detailed explanations help for “hard” questions, but for easier ones they may harm performance (excess verbosity becomes a drawback).
  • Demonstrates that improving model performance is not just about better solving (step-by-step), but improving how the question is understood and interpreted before solving.
